1. SARS (2002–2003) and MERS (2012) Origins  
  • Two criteria established natural spillover for both viruses:  
    – Identification of virus (or antibodies) in both humans and reservoir animals.  
    – Higher seropositivity among people with occupational animal exposures vs. the general population.  
  • SARS evidence:  
    – Palm civets in a Guangdong market harbored viruses 99.8 percent identical to human SARS-CoV.  
    – 80 percent of those civets had SARS-CoV antibodies.  
    – A Guangdong serosurvey of ~800 people found 73 percent seropositivity in masked‐palm‐civet traders.  
    – Whole‐genome sequencing by the Chinese SARS Molecular Epidemiology Consortium identified two early human viral genotypes matching civet/farm samples.  
  • MERS evidence:  
    – Dromedary camels across the Middle East, Africa, and parts of Asia tested positive for MERS-CoV antibodies, including archived samples back decades.  
    – A 2012–2013 Saudi Arabia serosurvey of >10,000 adults showed 0.15 percent overall MERS-CoV antibody prevalence; camel shepherds were 15× higher and slaughterhouse workers 23× higher.  

2. COVID-19 (SARS-CoV-2) Origins: Gaps in Natural-Spillover Evidence  
  • Gao et al. (peer‐review pending) sampled 457 animals of 18 species in Wuhan, early 2020: zero tested positive for SARS-CoV-2.  
  • No studies have shown higher seropositivity among Wuhan animal‐market workers compared to the general population. Early serosurveys omitted occupational data.  
  • Worobey et al. and Pekar et al. present geospatial and phylogenetic analyses suggesting the Huanan Seafood Market as the spillover site—but Kahn argues these data are equally compatible with:  
    1) Human‐to‐market introduction and local amplification, or  
    2) Natural spillover elsewhere then market amplification.  
  • Gao et al. report no correlation between animal‐seller locations or human‐density spots and positive environmental samples, supporting the “market as amplifier” hypothesis.  

3. Laboratory-Leak Hypothesis as a Viable Alternative  
  • Laboratory accidents occur frequently. In the most recent CDC/USDA Select Agent Program year:  
    – 205 select‐agent theft/loss/release reports (≈4 incidents/week).  
    – 196 releases; 177 indicating potential occupational exposures.  
  • Kahn calls for:  
    1) Serological sampling from 2019–early 2020 including occupation and location (market vs. lab workers).  
    2) Full transparency on 2015–2019 SARS-related coronavirus research in Wuhan (samples, sequences, records).  

4. Conclusion  
  • Neither natural‐spillover nor lab‐leak hypotheses can yet be ruled in or out.  
  • Objective data and comprehensive investigations are essential to determine SARS-CoV-2’s origin and to better prevent future pandemics.
